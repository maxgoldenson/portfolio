Shane's Barbershop Clipper Guard

A commercially deployed product — designed in collaboration with a working barber, optimized for additive manufacturing at small scale, and shipped in approximately 500 units to professional barbers.

01 — Problem

A barber's tool that didn't exist the way he needed it

Shane, a local barber, had a specific problem: the clipper guards available on the market weren't quite right for how he worked. The existing guards didn't grip reliably, didn't hold their size setting under pressure, or wore out too quickly for a professional who uses them all day.

The challenge was translating his feedback — which was entirely qualitative ("it slips", "it feels cheap", "it doesn't stay put") — into concrete engineering requirements that could drive a real design.

"He knew exactly what was wrong. He couldn't tell me the dimensions, tolerances, or materials he needed. That translation was the job."

02 — Process

From qualitative feedback to a manufacturable design

01

User research & requirements translation

Spent time in the barbershop observing how guards are used, worn, and removed. Converted each qualitative complaint into a measurable requirement: "it slips" → minimum retention force spec; "feels cheap" → minimum wall thickness and surface finish; "doesn't stay put" → snap fit preload requirement.

02

Geometry design + DFM for FDM

Designed the guard in SolidWorks with additive manufacturing constraints from the start: no unsupported overhangs above 45°, wall thicknesses above minimum printable feature size, snap features oriented along the print axis for maximum strength. Avoided post-processing requirements.

03

Iterative prototyping with the end user

Printed prototype revisions and brought them directly to Shane for feedback in-use. Each iteration addressed specific issues — grip texture, snap retention, dimensional fit to the clipper blade. Ran ~6 design iterations before reaching a design Shane was willing to sell under his brand.

04

Production scaling and quality control

Set up a production print workflow with consistent slicer settings, material specification, and a simple go/no-go dimensional check. Delivered production batches with a defect rate low enough for commercial sale.

03 — Solution

A guard that professionals actually want

The final design addresses all of Shane's original complaints: a textured grip surface, a snap fit retention system with measured preload, and wall geometry that resists flexing under use. The design is also modular — multiple guard sizes share the same snap interface, reducing the design and tooling burden for a full set.

The product carries Shane's Barbershop branding and is sold directly to his clients and other barbers — making this a real commercial product, not just a portfolio piece.
