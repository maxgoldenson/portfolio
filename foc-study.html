FOC Motor Control Study

01 — Problem

Using motor controllers without understanding them

Robotics and mechatronics projects routinely rely on off-the-shelf ESCs and motor drivers — but treating them as black boxes limits your ability to tune them, debug them, or design systems that push their limits. At Brewbird, grinder motor control directly affected taste; in FRC, drive motor behavior affected match outcomes.

I wanted to understand FOC deeply enough to implement it myself — not just use it. That meant starting from first principles: what is a rotating magnetic field, how does a three-phase winding produce it, and how do you control torque independent of speed?

"Reading a library's source code teaches you more about motor control than any tutorial. Writing your own teaches you everything else."

02 — Process

Experiment-driven learning

01

Clarke & Park transform derivation

Derived the Clarke transform (3-phase → 2-axis stationary) and Park transform (stationary → rotating reference frame) from first principles. Verified numerically in Python before implementing in firmware.

02

Open-loop SVPWM implementation

Implemented Space Vector PWM on a microcontroller driving a 3-phase gate driver. Verified correct phase relationships on an oscilloscope before closing any loops.

03

Rotor position sensing

Integrated a magnetic encoder for rotor angle. Validated angle reading against known positions. This is the feedback that makes field orientation possible — without accurate angle, FOC degrades to open-loop commutation.

04

Current control loops (Id / Iq)

Implemented the d-axis and q-axis PI controllers that are the heart of FOC. Tuned gains empirically, observing torque response and current waveforms. Experimented with flux weakening for extended speed range.

03 — Solution

A working FOC implementation — and deep intuition

The outcome isn't a product — it's foundational knowledge. A working FOC firmware stack, a set of validated experiments, and the ability to reason from first principles about any motor control problem: why a motor coggs, what limits peak torque, how back-EMF changes with speed.

This directly improved my ability to specify, tune, and debug motor systems in real products at Brewbird — particularly the grinder motor where torque consistency affects taste.
