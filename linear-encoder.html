Linear Capacitive Encoder

A sub-$5 high-accuracy linear position sensor achieving ±10μm resolution — designed from theory to validated prototype because the market had no good solution at this size and price.

Context
Brewbird — Grinder Module

01 — Problem

No sensor fit the constraints

At Brewbird, the coffee grinder module required precise linear position feedback to reliably control grind size — which directly determines taste. The existing solution was inconsistent, leading to grind variance and unpredictable brew quality.

Off-the-shelf linear encoders either were too large to fit the mechanism, too expensive to deploy at scale, or simply not accurate enough for the ±10μm tolerance the application demanded. There was a genuine gap in the market.

"When existing solutions lacked a balance of size and accuracy, I had to develop a novel approach from first principles."

02 — Process

From theory to prototype

Capacitive sensing was the right approach — it's inherently non-contact, robust to contamination, and cheap to implement on a PCB. The core principle: two overlapping conductive plates form a capacitor whose value changes predictably as they slide relative to each other.

01

Theoretical modeling

Derived the capacitance-vs-displacement relationship from first principles. Modeled fringe fields and parasitic capacitance that would affect accuracy at small scales.

02

PCB geometry design

Designed the electrode geometry to maximize sensitivity and minimize cross-axis error. Iterated layout in EasyEDA, optimizing trace width, spacing, and guard ring placement to reject environmental noise.

03

Prototype fabrication

Ordered PCB prototypes and assembled the capacitive sensing circuit. Used a microcontroller with a capacitance-to-digital converter IC and wrote firmware to read and filter position data.

04

Validation & characterization

Built a test fixture to measure encoder output against a ground-truth reference. Characterized linearity, hysteresis, and repeatability across temperature and humidity. Iterated on shielding and signal conditioning until ±10μm was reliably achieved.

03 — Solution

A novel sensor for under $5

The final design is a two-PCB capacitive linear encoder — a stationary sensing PCB and a moving electrode PCB that travels with the grinder mechanism. The design fits within the existing mechanical envelope with no changes to surrounding structure.

Signal conditioning and filtering firmware running on the ESP32 converts raw capacitance readings to a linearized position output at high update rates. The system is non-contact, wear-free, and inherently immune to lubricant contamination — all critical for a food-adjacent product.
