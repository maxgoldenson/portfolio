2023 Robot — Pain d'Épi

As Technical Captain of FRC Team 5940, I led the design of a low-hysteresis robotic arm using a novel Belt-box drive, validated wire routing to 10,000 torsional cycles, and applied FEA to lightweight critical gears — finishing as Curie Division Finalists.

01 — Problem

Building a competitive robot in 6 weeks — with no margin for error

FRC (First Robotics Competition) gives teams exactly 6 weeks to design, build, and program a robot capable of competing at the national level. In 2023, the challenge required precise game-piece manipulation at multiple heights — demanding a reliable, high-reach arm mechanism.

Two specific engineering problems defined the design: how to transmit power across a rotating joint without hysteresis or backlash, and how to run power wires through that same pivot without them failing under constant torsional cycling.

"Chain has chordal action — it creates speed pulses as each link engages the sprocket. For a precision arm, that's unacceptable."

02 — Process

Engineering each subsystem from first principles

01

Architecture selection (source-of-truth deck)

As Technical Captain, I created an evolving "source of truth" slide deck to reason through robot architectures using layout sketches and block CAD. This showed teammates not just what we decided — but why, preventing backtracking and aligning the team quickly.

02

Belt-box arm drive (eliminating chain)

Designed a unique "Belt-box" — a compact gearbox using belts instead of chain — to transmit torque across the arm pivot without chordal action. Belts run smoothly at any tooth engagement, giving the arm linear, predictable response.

03

Wire routing through the pivot + torsional test jig

Routing power wires through the arm pivot is the cleanest cable management solution, but those wires see aggressive torsional cycling every match. I built a custom jig that rotated test wires to 10,000 cycles without failure — proving the design's margin of safety before it went on the robot.

04

FEA-driven swerve gear lightweighting

Used SOLIDWORKS FEA to simulate stress in the swerve drive gears and identify material that could be safely removed. Described forces, characterized materials, reviewed stress contours, and iterated the design. Then machined the gears on our CNC using a custom aluminum fixture I also designed.

03 — Solution

A uniquely architected, tested robot

The final robot featured a slanted extension and arm with the Belt-box drive, pivot-routed and validated wiring, and lightweighted CNC-machined swerve gears. Every major design decision was documented with its rationale — making it reproducible and teachable for the rest of the team.

The robot performed reliably throughout competition, with no mechanical failures on the arm or drivetrain — a direct result of the validation work done before competition.
