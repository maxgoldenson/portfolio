High-Speed CoreXY Printer CAD

A full CAD study for a record-speed CoreXY 3D printer — designed around the physics of high-acceleration motion, with every structural and mass decision justified by dynamics, not convention.

01 — Problem

What actually limits print speed?

3D print speed is limited by two things: how fast plastic can be melted (volumetric flow rate) and how fast the toolhead can accelerate and decelerate without the frame resonating or the head overshooting. Most printers compromise heavily on the second — stiff frames are heavy, heavy toolheads need slow acceleration.

The CoreXY motion system offers a structural advantage: both motors are stationary, so the moving mass is only the toolhead. This makes high acceleration theoretically achievable — if the rest of the machine is designed with that goal in mind from the start.

"Most printer frames are designed to be 'good enough.' A speed-optimized design means every gram and every stiffness path is intentional."

02 — Process

Designing around dynamics, not aesthetics

The design process started from structural requirements, not visual conventions. Each assembly decision was preceded by a question: what load path does this carry, and what's the minimum material to carry it reliably?

Key design decisions included: using aluminum extrusion in tension/compression orientations only (never bending), minimizing toolhead mass by relocating the extruder motor to the frame (Bowden-adjacent design), designing gantry rails for maximum second moment of area per gram, and providing intentional vibration isolation between the bed and the frame.

The CAD also included a detailed BOM with mass accounting — every part was weighed on paper before it was modeled in full detail.

03 — Solution

A fully specified, dynamics-first design

The result is a complete CAD package — frame, gantry, toolhead, bed, electronics layout — designed to a specific acceleration target. The toolhead is one of the lightest in the design space while retaining a direct-drive extruder option.

This is a concept study, not a built machine — the value is in the design methodology and the CAD craft: clean parametric models, proper mate structure, and a design that could be handed to a manufacturer today.

A template for dynamics-first mechanical design

The methodology developed here — starting from load paths, mass targets, and dynamic requirements before modeling anything — is directly applicable to any high-performance mechanical system. It informed how I approach mechanism design at Brewbird, where every gram added to a moving assembly has a cost in motor sizing and power consumption.
