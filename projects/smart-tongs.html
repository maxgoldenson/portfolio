Smart Tongs — Food Waste Reduction

A fully custom ESP32 IoT device — with a bespoke PCB, precision encoder, and wireless connectivity — designed to reduce student food waste by making consumption visible in real time. Won a sustainability award.

01 — Problem

Students over-serve. No one notices until it's too late.

Food waste in cafeteria settings is largely driven by oversized initial servings. There's no real-time feedback at the point of serving to nudge behavior — signage and portion guides are passive and ignored.

We needed something embedded in the act of serving itself — unobtrusive, immediate, and quantifiable.

"Students need a way to be mindful of their consumption habits — at the moment it matters, not after."

02 — Process

System design from hardware to wireless

As electronics lead, I owned the full hardware stack: sensor selection, schematic capture, PCB layout, firmware, and power management.

Serving measurement
Magnetic quadrature encoder on the hinge, accurate to 0.1°. Combined with an MPU-6050 IMU to distinguish deliberate serving motions from idle handling.
Custom PCB (TEU)
Designed the Tong Electronics Unit in EasyEDA — ESP32-S3, battery management IC, auto wake-up circuit, and all peripherals on a board sized to fit inside the tong handle.
Power management
30-day battery life via aggressive sleep modes and a motion-triggered wake circuit fed by the IMU. Smart voltage monitoring and temperature shutoff protect the 2200mAh Li-ion cell.
User feedback
OLED display shows current and cumulative serving data. Rumble motor and piezo buzzer provide haptic and audio cues when a threshold is exceeded.
PCB Design

Schematic to verified layout

The schematic captures the full signal and power chain — from the ESP32-S3 through encoder inputs, IMU, display driver, and BMS — before layout began. Routing priority went to power planes and high-speed signal isolation.

TEU — Tong Electronics Unit

Fabricated and populated board

The fabricated TEU REV 1.3 measures to fit inside the tong handle. All key components — ESP32-S3 module, BMS IC, encoder connector, and OLED header — are visible on the populated board.

Mechanical Assembly

Integration into the tong body

The mechanical challenge was fitting all electronics into standard tong geometry without modifying the serving experience. The encoder mounts at the hinge axle, the TEU slides into the handle cavity, and the OLED is flush-mounted on the outer arm face.

03 — Solution

A fully integrated smart serving device

The device looks and feels like a normal pair of tongs. The TEU PCB is hidden inside the handle, the encoder is embedded in the hinge axle, and the OLED is visible on one arm. WiFi relay to a base station enables aggregate reporting with no wired connection.

05 — Key Learning

Power budget forces every design decision

Designing for 30-day battery life on a physically handled device is a different problem than designing for low average power. Every peripheral, every ISR, every network transaction had to be evaluated against the budget. This constraint made the design better — it eliminated convenience features that would have been noise, and forced aggressive sleep mode design that proved the hardware's real capabilities.

Owning the full hardware stack — from sensor selection through schematic capture through firmware — meant that tradeoffs between power, response time, and accuracy were always explicit, never inherited from a dev board's assumptions.
